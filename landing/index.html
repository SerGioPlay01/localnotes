Origin Story

Why Local Notes was built

It started with a simple frustration — finding a notes app that was truly private, fast, and free.

The problem with existing apps
☁️
Everything syncs to a server. Notion, Evernote, Google Keep — your notes live on someone else's computer. One breach and your data is exposed.
💳
Encryption costs money. Most apps lock real encryption behind a paid plan. Privacy shouldn't be a premium feature.
📋
Registration walls everywhere. Just to write a note you need an email, a password, and to agree to a privacy policy that lets them use your data.
🌐
No offline support. Most web-based apps are useless without internet. Your notes should be available anywhere, always.
The search begins
Looking for a private notes app
Tried dozens of apps. Every option either required an account, synced to a cloud, lacked encryption, or charged for basic features. Nothing felt truly private.
The realization
The browser already has everything needed
Modern browsers have IndexedDB for local storage, the Web Crypto API for AES-256 encryption, and Service Workers for offline support. No server needed.
First prototype
Built a minimal working version
A simple editor, local storage, basic encryption. No frameworks, no dependencies. Just HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript. It worked — and it was fast.
Growing the idea
Tags, calendar, 12 languages, PWA
Added rich text editing, color tags, a built-in calendar, transliteration search, and full localization for 12 languages. Packaged as a PWA for native-like installation.
Today — v1.1.0
Open source & free for everyone
Local Notes is MIT-licensed and free forever. The goal is simple: give everyone a secure, private place to write — without asking for anything in return.
🔒
The answer: keep everything in the browser
Local Notes uses your browser as a secure vault. AES-256 encryption, IndexedDB storage, Service Worker for offline — all built into modern browsers. No server, no account, no compromise.

The .note Format

Your data, your format

The .note file is a portable, encrypted container designed from the ground up with one goal: your notes belong to you — not to any platform, cloud, or company.

🔒
Born from a privacy problem
Every popular notes app stores your data on remote servers. One breach, one subpoena, one policy change — and your private thoughts are exposed. The .note format was created to make that impossible by design.
📦
What's inside a .note file
A .note file is a self-contained JSON bundle: your note content, metadata, tags, and — if encryption is enabled — an AES-256 ciphertext that only your password can unlock. No proprietary binary format, no vendor lock-in.
🌐
Offline-first, always portable
Export any note as a .note file and carry it on a USB drive, send it over Signal, or archive it for decades. Import it back into Local Notes on any device — no account, no internet required.
🛡️
Zero-knowledge by default
When you encrypt a note, the key never leaves your device. Local Notes uses the browser's built-in Web Crypto API — the same standard used by banks. Even if someone intercepts your .note file, they see only ciphertext.

FAQ

Common questions

Yes, completely. Local Notes is MIT-licensed open source software. There are no paid plans, no premium tiers, no hidden fees — ever. All features are available to everyone for free.

Your notes are stored in your browser's IndexedDB — a local database built into every modern browser. Nothing is ever sent to a server. Your data never leaves your device.

Clearing browser storage will delete your notes. We recommend exporting your notes regularly as .note files, HTML, or Markdown. You can re-import .note files at any time to restore your data.

When you enable encryption, your notes are encrypted using the Web Crypto API built into your browser. A key derived from your password is used to encrypt each note before it's saved. Without the correct password, the data is unreadable — even if someone accesses your device.

Local Notes is intentionally local-only — there's no sync by design. To move notes between devices, export them as .note files and import on the other device. This keeps your data fully under your control.
